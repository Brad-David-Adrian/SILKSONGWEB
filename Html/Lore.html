The Kingdom's Fall: A Deep Dive

We have left behind the doomed kingdom of Hallownest. Now, it is time to uncover the story of Pharloom‚Äîa civilization that shone brightly only to fall victim to its own ambition and manipulation. Although Silksong's lore is less ambiguous than Hollow Knight's‚Äîlargely because Hornet, unlike the Knight, can speak‚Äîunderstanding exactly what happened to the Citadel and why the land has withered requires an in-depth analysis. Here, we explain the history of this kingdom and its inhabitants.

Basic Summary

The story begins with Hornet held prisoner by a caravan. A small luminous bug approaches her cage, breaking the seal that binds her. Upon freeing herself, she destroys the caravan and the insects guarding it. She finds herself in a strange land called Pharloom (formerly referred to in translations as Telalejana). Her main objective is to discover who her captors were, why they captured her, and under whose orders they served.

The Citadel

Everything in Pharloom revolves around the Citadel, the kingdom's economic, political, and religious center. All insects in the land feel a compulsion to make a pilgrimage to this place, promised that upon arrival, they will find a land of light and riches devoid of suffering. However, the route is perilous, and only the 'worthy' may cross its gates.

When Hornet arrives in Pharloom, she immediately suspects the true nature of the Citadel. The path is teeming with aggressive insects, victims of a madness‚Äîoften called the "Silk's Curse"‚Äîthat causes them to lose their minds during their pilgrimage and attack others. She also discovers that while the Citadel has built rest sites, using them requires payment in Rosaries, the currency of this kingdom.

Upon reaching the Citadel, we discover that it is no paradise. The capital of Pharloom has fallen. It is overrun by bugs affected by the curse, and the few pilgrims who manage to reach it must take refuge from dangers far greater than those in the wastelands below.

The Silk Mother and the Origin of the Weavers

In the universe of Hollow Knight and Silksong, gods are known as "Higher Beings." Just as the tragedy of Hallownest revolved around the Radiance, Pharloom's history originates from the Great Silk Mother.

The purpose of these gods is to be worshipped. She sought subjects but was unable to create what she considered 'true life' with her silk alone. Instead, she used it to evolve lesser arachnids, thus shaping the Weavers‚Äîa tribe of spiders with great power over silk who worshipped the Great Mother with their songs.

Over time, the Weavers longed to be free from their goddess. They wrapped her in a silk cocoon, forcing her into an eternal slumber. However, the power of a Higher Being is not easily contained. To maintain the seal, they founded the Citadel and a religion centered on pilgrimage. The prayers and songs of the pilgrims kept the goddess asleep, but the Weavers continued to feel her influence. Fearful, they hid and began experimenting, hoping to create artifacts that would pacify their creator forever and allow them to ascend.

Realizing their efforts were in vain, the vast majority of the Weavers abandoned Pharloom. To replace them, a new system of administration was implemented in the Citadel, led by Judges, Guardians, and Architects. Though they were aware of the false religion's true purpose, they were quickly corrupted by power and greed.

Greed and Corruption

The new leadership abused their power, extracting wealth from pilgrims and forcing endless labor under the guise of demonstrating faith. Evidence is everywhere: toll stations, paid rest stops, and confession booths requiring payment. Pilgrims labor endlessly for rewards that never come, while the elite live in luxury. The Judges even experimented on pilgrims, injecting silk into their bodies to extend their lives and bind them eternally, or extracting their life force to create animated servants.

The Fall of Pharloom

Pilgrims injected with silk began falling under the Mother's control. Many descended into madness, attacking their companions. The Citadel descended into chaos and collapsed completely. Only a few remained, mindlessly performing their duties out of blind faith. Yet the pilgrimages continued; their prayers kept the Mother bound. In her dreams, the goddess created two new beings: Lace and Ghost‚Äîfragile creatures of silk who question their own existence.

A Child's Resolve

Hornet is part Weaver, part Higher Being‚Äîtorn between helping Pharloom's inhabitants and succumbing to her divine nature to dominate. She respects local beliefs but remains critical of the Citadel. Sherma, a devout pilgrim, embodies how faith adapts to survive: rather than denying horrors, he reinterprets his beliefs to help others.

The Endings Explained

The Weaver Queen: Defeat the Mother directly. Hornet absorbs her divine power and succumbs to her Higher Being nature. Silk Binding: Help most inhabitants. Attempt to trap the Mother in the Abyss; the plan fails, and the Void invades Pharloom, showing that toppling oppression brings new horrors. Sister of the Void: Rescue Lace from the Abyss. Twisted Child: Hornet binds herself to the Mother, creating a new Higher Being. Age of Change: A mysterious ending tied to the Fungal Lord.

A Father's Love

Lace and Ghost lament their existence, never considering themselves truly alive. Lace believes she is the daughter the Mother truly desires. When the goddess is dragged into the Abyss with Lace, she shields her daughter with a cocoon, resisting the Void itself. This resistance causes earthquakes and devastation across Pharloom. Behind all the themes of imperialism, greed, and false religion lies a simpler truth: a parent will do anything to save their child‚Äîa recurring motif in the franchise that gives emotional weight to Silksong's sprawling tale.
